Predicting customer churn has become increasingly important as companies look to retain their customers and reduce the costs associated with acquiring new ones.

Preprocessing our data¶

One interesting observation we made when we explored the distribution of our numerical features is that they do not follow a normal distribution. This is particularly important when it comes to feature scaling since some scaling methods assume that the data follows a normal distribution.

Here, we chose to use MinMaxScaler instead of StandardScaler because MinMaxScaler scales the data to a range between 0 and 1 and is robust to outliers, whereas StandardScaler scales the data to have a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1. However, StandardScaler can be sensitive to outliers and may not perform well on non-Gaussian distributions.

Since our data has a non-Gaussian distribution, using MinMaxScaler is a better choice as it preserves the range of the original data while ensuring that all the features are on the same scale, which is a requirement for many machine learning algorithms.

Results and Comparisons¶

After training the random forest classifier with various hyperparameters, we obtained an accuracy of 0.790102, precision of 0.629738, recall of 0.471616, and F1 score of 0.539326. In an attempt to understand the importance of each feature in our model, we plotted the feature importances as shown above.

When comparing the importances of the features between the two models, we can see that they are quite different. For example, in the logistic regression model, the feature with the largest weight was tenure. Other features that had a strong association with customer churn in the logistic regression model included OnlineSecurity, TechSupport, and Contract.

In contrast, the feature with the highest importance in the random forest model was MonthlyCharges, with an importance score of 0.180809. This indicates that customers who pay higher monthly charges are more likely to churn. Other features that were important in the random forest model included TotalCharges, tenure, and Contract.

It is worth noting that the weights and importances of the features can vary between models and can be influenced by a range of factors, such as the size and quality of the data, the type of model used, and the hyperparameters chosen. Therefore, it is important to carefully consider the results of each model and interpret them in the context of the specific problem being addressed.
